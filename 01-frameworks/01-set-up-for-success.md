# Pillar 1: Set Up for Success

> The job of a product leader begins before any discovery session or sprint planning. It begins with creating the conditions for the team to do their best work.

---

## Why This Matters

Most product dysfunction — missed timelines, poor prioritisation, lack of team energy — can be traced back to a failure to set up properly. Teams that lack clarity on goals, context, or their own remit waste enormous amounts of time and goodwill on misaligned work.

Setting up for success is not a one-time activity. It needs to be revisited as the team evolves, as strategy shifts, and whenever a new person joins.

---

## The Four Foundations

### 1. Strategic Context

Every PM and team member should be able to answer: *"Why does our product exist, and what does winning look like?"*

If they can't, start here.

**Actions:**
- Establish a clear product vision (3–5 year horizon) that is genuinely inspiring and directionally stable
- Define company and product strategy: what you're choosing to do, and crucially, what you're choosing *not* to do
- Map how the product vision connects to business objectives (OKRs or equivalent)
- Make this visible — a one-pager, a Notion page, a printed poster. Invisible context doesn't exist

**Signals that this is working:**
- Team members can articulate the "why" unprompted
- Scope debates resolve more quickly because the vision acts as a filter
- New joiners reach productive context faster

---

### 2. Goal Clarity

Vague goals produce vague outcomes. Good goals are specific, measurable, and connected to something customers actually care about.

**The Goal-Setting Hierarchy:**

```
Company Objective (annual)
    └── Product Objective (quarterly)
            └── Team Goal (sprint / cycle)
                    └── Individual Focus
```

**Best practices:**
- Use outcome-based goals, not output-based. "Increase trial-to-paid conversion by 15%" is better than "ship onboarding flow v2"
- Separate lagging metrics (what we're trying to change) from leading indicators (signals we're on track)
- Review goals at the start and end of every cycle — not just at the annual review
- Goals should be a filter for prioritisation, not an afterthought

**Common failure modes:**
- Too many goals (no focus)
- Goals that measure activity, not impact
- Goals set top-down without team input (creates compliance, not commitment)

---

### 3. Role Clarity and Team Design

Ambiguity about who owns what creates duplicated effort, missed handoffs, and political friction.

**For each team member, make explicit:**
- What they own (their accountability surface)
- What decisions they can make autonomously
- What decisions require escalation or input
- How their work connects to team goals

**Useful tools:**
- **DACI / RACI** for decision rights on specific decisions (see [Decision-Making Frameworks](03-decision-making-frameworks.md))
- **Team charter** — a lightweight document that captures team mission, ways of working, and escalation paths
- **1:1 structure** — regular, structured conversations that combine delivery check-ins with growth and wellbeing

---

### 4. Stakeholder Alignment

Product teams don't operate in isolation. Misaligned stakeholders create noise, slow decisions, and erode trust.

**Stakeholder mapping:**

| Stakeholder | Interest | Influence | Engagement Mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Executive sponsor | Strategy & ROI | High | Monthly update + async |
| Engineering lead | Feasibility & resourcing | High | Weekly sync |
| Commercial / Sales | Pipeline & messaging | Medium | Fortnightly + ad hoc |
| Design | UX quality & consistency | High | Embedded in team |
| Data / Analytics | Measurement | Medium | On demand + sprint reviews |

**Key behaviours:**
- Over-communicate with high-influence stakeholders; don't wait to be asked
- Establish a regular cadence of written updates (weekly or fortnightly) so people feel informed without needing meetings
- Surface tensions and disagreements proactively — surprises erode trust far more than problems do

---

## New PM Onboarding Checklist

When joining a new team or product, use this to get grounded in the first 30 days:

- [ ] Read all available strategy docs, OKRs, and product specs
- [ ] Map the stakeholder landscape and schedule 1:1s with key people
- [ ] Understand current metrics and baselines — what is actually being tracked?
- [ ] Shadow customer calls or support tickets to hear the customer voice directly
- [ ] Identify the three biggest open questions or risks facing the product
- [ ] Clarify your own role, decision rights, and immediate priorities with your manager
- [ ] Produce a 30/60/90 day plan and share it for feedback
