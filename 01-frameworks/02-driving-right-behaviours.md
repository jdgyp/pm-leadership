# Pillar 2: Driving Right Behaviours

> You can't mandate great product thinking. But you can create an environment where it's the natural default.

---

## Why This Matters

The behaviours of a product team — how they prioritise, how they handle uncertainty, how they collaborate, how they respond to feedback — determine product outcomes far more than any framework or process. Leadership's job is to make the right behaviours easy and the wrong ones hard.

This is not about policing behaviour. It's about building a culture through consistency, accountability, and modelling what good looks like.

---

## The Behaviour Stack

Think of team behaviours in three layers:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────┐
│         SHARED MINDSETS          │  "How we think about our work"
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│         TEAM HABITS              │  "What we do consistently"
├──────────────────────────────────┤
│         INDIVIDUAL ACCOUNTABILITY│  "What each person owns"
└──────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each layer reinforces the others. Habits encode mindsets. Accountability structures make habits stick.

---

## Shared Mindsets

These are the ways of thinking that should be visibly reinforced in meetings, retrospectives, and written communication:

**Customer obsession over internal opinion**
Debates about what to build should be resolved by evidence, not seniority. The most senior person in the room is not the customer. Create space for data and customer voice to lead.

**Outcomes over output**
Shipping is never the goal. The goal is behaviour change — in customers, in usage metrics, in business KPIs. Teams should celebrate outcomes, not features.

**Honest uncertainty**
Good product teams say "I don't know" freely and treat it as the starting point for learning, not a failure to be hidden. Psychological safety is a prerequisite.

**Continuous improvement**
Every cycle should leave the team slightly better than the last — on the product, on the process, on their working relationships. Retrospectives should produce real changes, not just discussion.

## Team Habits

Consistent, well-run rituals are the mechanism through which mindsets become embedded. Each ritual should have a clear owner, a clear purpose, and a standing agenda.

### Discovery Rhythm

| Ritual | Cadence | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Customer interview debrief | Weekly | Share insights from discovery; surface themes |
| Metric review | Fortnightly | Review leading indicators; identify anomalies |
| Assumption mapping | Monthly | Challenge what we believe to be true about users |

### Delivery Rhythm

| Ritual | Cadence | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Sprint / cycle planning | Per cycle | Align on what we're building and why |
| Mid-cycle check-in | Per cycle | Catch blockers early; re-prioritise if needed |
| Retrospective | Per cycle | Honest review of what worked and what didn't |

### Alignment Rhythm

| Ritual | Cadence | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Stakeholder update | Fortnightly | Keep key stakeholders informed; reduce noise |
| Cross-functional sync | Weekly | Unblock dependencies between product, eng, design |
| Leadership review | Monthly | Connect team progress to company strategy |

**Key ritual principles:**
- Every meeting needs a clear owner and a defined output
- Meetings that produce no decisions or actions should be cut or converted to async
- Retrospectives must result in at least one concrete change per cycle

## Individual Accountability

Accountability is not punishment. It's clarity about what each person is responsible for, and follow-through when they commit to something.

**How to build it:**

**Public commitments**
At the start of each cycle, individuals state what they're working on and what success looks like. This is shared with the team, not just the manager. Peer accountability is often more powerful than hierarchical accountability.

**Consistent follow-through**
When someone commits to a deliverable or decision and doesn't follow through, that needs to be addressed — privately first, then more directly if the pattern persists. Tolerating persistent under-delivery normalises it.

**Recognition that reinforces the right things**
Call out examples of good product thinking, not just delivery speed. Recognise someone for a great discovery insight, a well-reasoned prioritisation decision, or exceptional stakeholder communication.

**Constructive challenge**
Leaders should model the behaviour of politely but directly challenging assumptions. "What evidence supports that?" and "Have we spoken to customers about this?" should be normal, comfortable questions — not threatening ones.

## Feedback Culture

Feedback is the mechanism by which teams improve. Without it, the same mistakes repeat.

**The feedback ladder:**

```
1. Observe     → Note a specific behaviour or outcome
2. Impact      → Describe why it matters (to the customer, team, or outcome)
3. Request     → Ask for a different behaviour or approach
4. Dialogue    → Create space for the other person to respond
```

**Leader behaviours that build a feedback culture:**
- Ask for feedback on your own performance, openly and specifically
- Respond to feedback with gratitude, not defensiveness
- Close the loop — tell people what you did with their input
- Distinguish between feedback on the work (always appropriate) and feedback on the person (requires more care)

## Anti-Patterns to Watch For

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | How to Address It |
|---|---|---|
| HiPPO decisions | The Highest Paid Person's Opinion wins by default | Require evidence-based framing; create space for dissent |
| Feature factory mentality | Team celebrates shipping, not learning | Reframe success metrics in retros and planning |
| Over-commitment | Team agrees to everything, delivers on little | Introduce explicit capacity planning and "not this cycle" language |
| Silent disagreement | Issues surface in 1:1s but not in the room | Normalise structured debate in group settings |
| Retrospective theatre | Retros happen but nothing changes | Assign owners to every action; review previous actions first |
