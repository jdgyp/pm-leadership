# Case Study 2: Shipping with Cross-Functional Alignment

> **Pillars in focus:** Driving Right Behaviours · Set Up for Success

---

## Situation

A growth-stage startup was preparing to launch a major new product tier — the most significant release in the company's history. The PM, designer, and engineering team had been working on it for four months and were two weeks from the intended launch date.

Then, ten days before launch, the Head of Sales flagged that the pricing model didn't match what the sales team had been telling prospects. The Head of Marketing hadn't seen the final feature set and had concerns about positioning. Legal had a question about data handling that had been asked three months earlier and never resolved.

Launch was delayed by six weeks. Two key enterprise prospects went quiet. The post-mortem was painful.

The question wasn't *what went wrong* — it was obvious in hindsight. The question was: *how do you prevent this on every future launch?*

---

## Diagnosis

The PM lead — brought in after this incident — interviewed every function involved. The themes were consistent:

- **No shared definition of "launch-ready."** Each team had different criteria in their heads, and no one had ever written them down together.
- **Information asymmetry.** Engineering and product were sharing updates within the squad, but commercial, marketing, and legal were getting information late or through informal channels.
- **No one owned cross-functional alignment.** Everyone assumed someone else was coordinating. No one was.
- **Decisions were made without the right people in the room.** The pricing model had been decided in a product/commercial meeting that legal and customer success weren't invited to.

The root cause wasn't bad intentions — it was a missing operating model for cross-functional work.

---

## Intervention

### Step 1: Define "launch-ready" together

The PM lead ran a two-hour working session with leads from product, engineering, design, commercial, marketing, legal, and customer success. The single agenda item: *What needs to be true for us to be ready to launch?*

Each function named their criteria. These were compiled into a shared **Launch Readiness Checklist** — a living document that any future launch would be measured against.

Excerpts from the final checklist:

| Function | Criteria |
|---|---|
| Product | Success metrics defined and baseline captured |
| Engineering | Load testing passed; rollback plan documented |
| Legal | Data handling reviewed and signed off |
| Marketing | Positioning brief complete; launch assets approved |
| Commercial | Sales team trained; objection handling prepared |
| Customer Success | Support documentation live; escalation path agreed |

The act of creating this together — not just circulating a document — was as important as the output. Each team saw what the others needed, which surfaced several assumptions that had been causing invisible friction.

**Principle applied:** Cross-functional alignment & influence, Set Up for Success

---

### Step 2: Create a launch pod

For subsequent launches, the PM lead established a **launch pod**: a lightweight cross-functional group that owned launch readiness together, not just within their own function.

**How it worked:**
- Formed 8 weeks before target launch
- Weekly 30-minute sync with leads from each function
- A shared tracker updated async between sessions
- A single Slack channel for launch-related communications (replacing scattered DMs and email threads)
- A designated **Launch Driver** (usually the PM) responsible for ensuring nothing fell through the gaps — not doing everything, but making sure owners were clear and blockers were escalated

**DACI for the launch decision:**

| Role | Owner |
|---|---|
| Driver | PM lead |
| Approver | CPO |
| Contributors | Engineering, Design, Commercial, Marketing, Legal, CS leads |
| Informed | Board / exec team |

**Principle applied:** Driving right behaviours, Working frameworks

---

### Step 3: Build information rituals

Rather than relying on ad hoc communication, the PM lead introduced two standing rituals:

**Weekly written launch update** (async, 200 words max):
- What's been completed this week
- What's at risk
- Decisions needed from whom

Sent every Friday to all launch pod members and key stakeholders. This replaced the majority of "can you give me an update?" messages and meant no one was surprised at the weekly sync.

**Fortnightly stakeholder review** (30 minutes, optional attendance):
- Walk through the launch tracker together
- Surface and resolve any blockers
- Confirm or adjust the launch date based on readiness

**Principle applied:** Driving right behaviours

---

### Step 4: Normalise early escalation

The previous culture had been to hold issues internally and surface them only when they were critical. The PM lead explicitly modelled a different behaviour: raise problems early, in the open, without blame.

In the first launch pod meeting after the new structure was established, she named a risk she was personally uncertain about — the pricing model alignment — and asked the commercial lead to walk the group through it. This surfaced a genuine misalignment early, which was resolved in that session rather than two weeks before launch.

Visibly naming uncertainty and inviting others to fill gaps became a habit. Within a few cycles, other team members were doing the same without prompting.

**Principle applied:** Driving right behaviours — honest uncertainty, feedback culture

---

## Outcome

The next major launch — a similar scope to the one that had been delayed — shipped on the originally planned date. Post-launch:

- Zero cross-functional blockers surfaced in the final two weeks (versus four critical ones on the previous launch)
- Sales team reported feeling "genuinely prepared" for customer conversations
- Legal sign-off was completed three weeks before launch, not three days
- The PM lead received feedback from the CPO that the launch was "the smoothest we've ever run"

The launch readiness checklist became a company-wide standard, adopted by two other product teams.

---

## Key Lessons

**Alignment is a process, not a meeting.** One kick-off call doesn't create alignment. Consistent, structured communication over weeks does.

**Shared ownership requires explicit structure.** "Everyone is responsible" means no one is responsible. Naming a Driver, defining contributors, and agreeing a single Approver removes ambiguity.

**Model the behaviour you want to see.** The fastest way to create a culture of early escalation is for the most senior person in the room to do it first, without embarrassment.

**The checklist earns its value in creation, not just in use.** The conversation that produces a shared launch checklist is where misalignments surface and get resolved. The document itself is just the record.
