# Case Study 1: Realigning a Drifting Roadmap

> **Pillars in focus:** Set Up for Success · Working & Decision-Making Frameworks

---

## Situation

A B2B SaaS product team had been shipping consistently for two years. On the surface, things looked healthy — sprint velocity was high, features were launching regularly, and engineering was well-resourced. But something was wrong.

Customer retention had plateaued. The sales team was increasingly telling prospects "that feature is coming soon" and then chasing the PM team for delivery dates. The roadmap had become a list of features requested by specific customers or pushed by commercial stakeholders, with no clear thread connecting them to outcomes.

When the PM lead joined the team, the first thing she heard in 1:1s was: *"I'm not sure what we're actually trying to achieve."*

---

## Diagnosis

Using the **Set Up for Success** framework, the PM lead conducted a structured audit across three areas:

### 1. Strategic Context Check

She asked five team members — independently — to write down the product's top priority for the next six months.

She got five different answers.

This wasn't a failure of intelligence or effort. The team had no shared, written, visible goal. Strategy existed in the heads of a few senior people and was transmitted inconsistently.

### 2. Roadmap Audit

She mapped every item on the current roadmap against two questions:
- Which customer outcome does this contribute to?
- What evidence supports this being the highest-value thing we could build?

Of 34 roadmap items, only 9 had clear answers to both questions. The rest were either stakeholder requests with no outcome attached, or ideas that had made it onto the roadmap through informal conversation rather than a structured prioritisation process.

### 3. Stakeholder Mapping

Interviews with key stakeholders revealed a pattern: the roadmap had become a proxy for internal influence. Teams that asked loudest or most frequently got their requests added. There was no explicit process for saying no.

---

## Intervention

The PM lead structured a reset across four weeks, using principles from all three pillars.

### Week 1: Establish shared goals

Working with the product director and engineering lead, she drafted a one-page product strategy document covering:
- The 12-month outcome goal (reduce 90-day churn by 15%)
- The two underlying hypotheses about why customers churned
- What the team was explicitly *not* prioritising for the next two quarters

This was reviewed with the broader team and revised based on their input. The final version was published to a shared wiki and referenced in every subsequent planning conversation.

**Principle applied:** Outcome-driven, Cross-functional alignment

---

### Week 2: Re-prioritise the roadmap

With a clear outcome goal in place, the team ran a prioritisation workshop using **RICE scoring** and the **Outcome-First Filter** from the Decision-Making Frameworks pillar.

Each roadmap item was assessed:
1. Does it address one of the two churn hypotheses? If not → moved to a "parking lot" for later review
2. Of the remaining items, what is the RICE score?

The team reduced the active roadmap from 34 items to 11. The 23 deprioritised items were documented with a one-line rationale and shared with stakeholders — proactively, before anyone asked.

**Principle applied:** Outcome-driven, Working frameworks

---

### Week 3: Rebuild stakeholder trust

The most politically sensitive part was communicating the changes to commercial stakeholders who had items removed from the roadmap.

The approach:
- Individual conversations before any group announcement
- Clear framing: "We're not saying your request was wrong — we're saying we can't do it effectively until we've addressed the core retention problem"
- A commitment to a regular fortnightly written update so stakeholders never felt uninformed

Two stakeholders pushed back initially. One accepted the rationale after seeing the churn data presented clearly. One escalated — which led to a productive conversation with the product director that actually reinforced the new prioritisation approach.

**Principle applied:** Cross-functional alignment & influence

---

### Week 4: Embed new habits

To prevent the roadmap from drifting again, the team established two new rituals:

1. **Monthly roadmap review**: A standing agenda item to check all active items against current evidence and the outcome goal. Any item that can no longer trace to the goal is flagged for discussion.

2. **Stakeholder request intake process**: A simple form and triage process for new requests. Requests are acknowledged within 48 hours, triaged against the outcome goals, and responded to with a clear decision and rationale within 2 weeks.

**Principle applied:** Driving right behaviours

---

## Outcome

Six months later:
- 90-day churn had dropped by 11% (target: 15% — on track)
- The team consistently cited having clear goals as a key improvement in retrospectives
- Stakeholder satisfaction with the product team improved markedly — despite fewer features being shipped
- The PM lead attributed the progress less to any single feature and more to the team's renewed ability to focus

---

## Key Lessons

**Direction matters more than velocity.** A team shipping fast in the wrong direction is hard to course-correct. Slowing down to establish clarity upfront pays compounding returns.

**Saying no is a skill, not a refusal.** The most impactful thing the PM lead did was create a transparent, principled process for declining requests. This converted frustrated stakeholders into informed ones.

**Write things down.** The absence of a documented strategy was the root cause of almost every downstream problem. Once the strategy existed on paper, many disagreements resolved themselves.
