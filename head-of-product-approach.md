# My approach to running product

**Head of Product perspective · Group PM / Principal background · Consumer, B2B & Platform products**

---

## Philosophy

**The best Heads of Product create clarity and calm in complex organisations.** In a large tech function - especially one embedded in an engineering-led environment - the real job is not to manage a roadmap. It is to give a team of talented PMs direction, space, and confidence to do their best work; and to give leadership the signal they need to make good decisions.

That means holding the strategic thread above the noise, being the person who makes the complex feel manageable, and earning credibility with engineering and delivery peers - not by assertion, but by consistency and outcomes.

## Six areas I focus on

| # | Area | Summary |
|---|------|---------|
| 1 | **Strategy alignment** | Connect the product portfolio to commercial and customer outcomes. Translate business priorities into a coherent product narrative that teams can build towards, and that leadership can trust. |
| 2 | **Team leadership & people** | Grow great PMs, not just great products. Set a clear bar for craft - rigorous discovery, outcome framing, sharp communication - and create the conditions for PMs to lead with confidence. |
| 3 | **Portfolio health & roadmaps** | Maintain a clear view across roadmaps, dependencies, and trade-offs. Spot conflicts early, escalate the right decisions, and ensure the portfolio is coherent - not just a collection of team backlogs. |
| 4 | **Stakeholder management & upward reporting** | Product leads on outcomes and prioritisation; collaborates on everything else. Managing up means giving leadership clean, timely signals. Managing across means earning Engineering, Delivery, and Architecture's respect through consistency and good judgment. |
| 5 | **Delivery rhythm, metrics & retrospectives** | Own the operating model: how work gets planned, tracked, reviewed, and learned from. Maintain a product calendar teams can rely on, ensure we are hitting targets, and build a retrospective culture that makes the team smarter over time. |
| 6 | **Cross-practice operating model & DevRel** | Design how Product, Engineering, Delivery, and Architecture function together - including internal developer experience and enablement. In a platform context, product must actively invest in developer relations: understanding how engineers consume our platforms, removing friction, and building trust with internal users. |

## Operating rhythm - a calendar teams can plan to

Every ritual earns its slot by serving one of three purposes: **alignment, decision, or energy**. Nothing else.

| Rhythm | Participants | Purpose |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| Daily standup | Each squad | Blockers and coordination - not status theatre |
| Weekly PM sync | HoP + PMs | Portfolio signal: progress, risk, what needs me |
| Fortnightly 1:1 | HoP + each PM | Career, craft, and what they wouldn't say in a group |
| Bi-weekly portfolio review | HoP + leads | Trade-offs, dependencies, decisions that need escalation |
| Monthly showcase | Team + stakeholders | Celebrate what shipped; build credibility externally |
| Monthly upward report | HoP → leadership | Outcomes vs targets, portfolio health, risks, asks |
| Quarterly planning | Full team + partners | Roadmap lock, OKR alignment, resource trade-offs |
| Quarterly retrospective | HoP + PMs | What did we learn? What do we change? |
| Annual away day | Full product team | Culture, strategy, energy - not a planning session |

## On metrics - hitting targets, not just shipping features

I apply different measurement approaches depending on the team's context, maturity, and proximity to the customer. The common thread: metrics must be chosen deliberately, owned by the team, and reviewed with the same rigour as the roadmap.

**Customer-facing or commercially-oriented teams → OKRs**
Cascaded from business goals, with quarterly check-ins that distinguish between progress on key results and changes to the underlying assumptions. I push teams to write OKRs that would be embarrassing to miss - not ones designed to be hit.

**Platform and internal-developer-facing teams → North star + supporting metrics**
A single north star (e.g. developer time-to-integration, platform adoption rate) supported by leading indicators. This is the right model for integration platform products where commercial metrics are one step removed from team actions.

**Teams in discovery or transformation → Outcome-based roadmaps with leading indicators**
Where output metrics are premature, I use outcome-based roadmap items ("reduce onboarding friction" not "build onboarding wizard") paired with leading indicators that signal we are moving in the right direction before lagging metrics confirm it.

Across all teams: I maintain a simple monthly view of targets vs actuals, surfaced in the upward report. If we are off-track, I want to name it early and with a view on why - not explain it after the quarter closes.

## Decision-making frameworks

Frameworks are thinking aids, not substitutes for judgment. I use them to create a shared language for decisions, reduce bias in prioritisation, and make the reasoning behind choices visible and challengeable.

### RICE / ICE - Prioritisation
**When:** Scoping a roadmap cycle with competing requests and limited resource.

**How:** Score each item on Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort. ICE is a lighter version for faster cycles. I use these to structure the conversation - not to let the score make the decision. A RICE score that surprises the room is the beginning of the discussion, not the end.

---

### Now / Next / Later + Horizon model - Strategic bets
**When:** Balancing incremental delivery against longer-term investment. Horizon model for executive conversations; Now/Next/Later for team-level roadmap visibility.

**How:** Horizon 1 = run the business, Horizon 2 = grow the business, Horizon 3 = create the future. I use this to ensure we are not 100% in H1 by default - which is what happens when there is no explicit conversation about strategic bets.

---

### DACI - Cross-practice decisions
**When:** Any decision that crosses team, practice, or org boundaries - especially with Engineering, Architecture, or Delivery.

**How:** Driver (one person), Approver (one person), Contributors (small set), Informed (everyone else). I push for DACI on any decision that has stalled or is being made in the wrong room. It makes accountability explicit without making it political.

---

### MoSCoW - Scope negotiation
**When:** Release planning, MVP scoping, or when a stakeholder is pushing for everything at once.

**How:** Must have / Should have / Could have / Won't have. Simple, fast, and stakeholder-friendly. Particularly useful in cross-practice planning sessions where engineering capacity needs to be defended without becoming adversarial.

## On visibility - knowing enough without losing the team

I need signal, not detail. The design question is: what does each PM need from me to do their best work, and what do I need from them to hold accountability without creating noise?

- **Weekly async updates** from PMs: progress, risk, what they need from me. Readable in two minutes.
- **Fortnightly 1:1s** structured around their agenda - with space for career, craft, and the things that don't surface in group settings.
- **Bi-weekly portfolio review:** a conversation about trade-offs and decisions, not a status report.
- **Monthly playback:** what shipped, what we learned, how it tracked against targets. Shared up and across.
- **Showcases** as the team's moment - not mine to present.

---

## On reporting upwards - signal, not noise

Leadership needs enough to make good decisions and maintain confidence in the function. My monthly upward report covers five things, in this order:

1. **Outcomes vs targets** - are we hitting what we said we would? If not, why, and what are we doing about it?
2. **Portfolio health** - what is in flight, what is at risk, what trade-offs have been made this period.
3. **What shipped** - a brief playback of the most significant deliveries and their early impact.
4. **Risks and escalations** - what I am watching, and what I need a decision on.
5. **Asks** - specific, bounded requests: resource, priority calls, air cover. Not vague asks for support.

The format is short - one page or a five-minute verbal brief - and consistent. Consistency matters more than completeness: a leadership team that receives the same structure every month learns to read it faster and trust it more.

## On internal DevRel - product's relationship with engineering as a user

For platform and integration products, engineering teams are users. That means all the same product disciplines apply: understanding their jobs-to-be-done, measuring adoption and friction, iterating on the developer experience.

In practice, internal DevRel means:

- **Regular developer feedback loops** - structured channels for internal engineers to surface friction, gaps, and ideas.
- **Developer-facing documentation and onboarding** as a product surface, not an afterthought.
- **Tracking adoption, time-to-integration, and developer satisfaction** as first-class metrics alongside commercial ones.
- **Treating engineering teams as partners in discovery** - not just recipients of releases.
- **Earning trust through consistency** - predictable releases, honest roadmaps, and visible responsiveness to feedback.

In an engineering-led culture, this is one of the highest-leverage things a product leader can do. If the engineers who use our platforms trust product, everything else gets easier.

---

## On earning a seat at the table in an engineering-led culture

Product has to earn credibility through behaviour - not by claiming it.

- Showing up to architectural conversations with a point of view, not just requirements.
- Being honest about what we don't know, and rigorous about how we find out.
- Making prioritisation decisions with clear, consistent rationale - no surprises.
- Co-designing ways of working rather than imposing a product framework.
- Using frameworks like DACI to make cross-practice decisions legible, not to win arguments.
- Delivering. Credibility is built over time through outcomes.

---

## What I am looking for

A team to grow and a function to shape. A product portfolio with genuine complexity - internal, customer-facing, or both. An engineering-led culture that values product thinking when it is earned. And the scope to build something that makes the organisation better, not just the backlog longer.
